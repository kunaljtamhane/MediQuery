How does Pembrolizumab interact with the PD1 pathway and its ligands?

[Sources Used: Local Document Database, PubMed Peer-Reviewed Literature, Web Search (Non-Peer-Reviewed)]

Direct Answer Summary Pembrolizumab binds to the PD-1 receptor and blocks its interaction with PD-L1 and PD-L2, thereby restoring the immune response against tumor cells.

Evidence-Aligned Explanation Pembrolizumab, a humanized monoclonal anti-PD1 antibody, functions by binding to the PD-1 receptor on T-cells, preventing its interaction with PD-L1 and PD-L2. This blockade removes the inhibitory signals that tumor cells use to evade the immune system, thus enhancing the anti-tumor T-cell response. This mechanism is crucial for the efficacy of pembrolizumab in various cancers, particularly those with higher mutational loads such as melanoma, NSCLC, and mismatch repair (MMR) deficient cancers.

What is the relationship between CTLA4, CD28, CD80, and T-cell activation?

[Sources Used: Local Document Database, PubMed Peer-Reviewed Literature, Web Search (Non-Peer-Reviewed)]

Direct Answer Summary CTLA4 competes with CD28 for binding to CD80 and CD86, thereby inhibiting T-cell activation.

Evidence-Aligned Explanation CTLA4 and CD28 share the same ligands, CD80 and CD86, which are expressed on antigen-presenting cells (APCs). CD28 is a co-stimulatory receptor that enhances T-cell activation when it binds to CD80 or CD86. In contrast, CTLA4 acts as an inhibitory receptor. It has a higher affinity for CD80 and CD86 than CD28. When CTLA4 binds to these ligands, it prevents CD28 from binding, thereby suppressing the co-stimulatory signal required for full T-cell activation. This competitive binding mechanism is crucial for regulating T-cell responses and maintaining immune tolerance.

How does PDL1 expression on neoplastic cells contribute to tumor immune evasion?

[Sources Used: Local Document Database, PubMed Peer-Reviewed Literature, Web Search (Non-Peer-Reviewed)]

Direct Answer Summary PDL1 expression on neoplastic cells contributes to tumor immune evasion by inducing T-cell exhaustion and promoting the differentiation of T-cells into induced Tregs, which suppress cytotoxic T-cell function.

Evidence-Aligned Explanation PDL1 can be induced on neoplastic cells in response to interferon gamma, leading to the engagement of PDL1 with PD1 on T-cells. This interaction induces T-cells to become induced Tregs, which then suppress cytotoxic T-cell function, thereby facilitating tumor immune evasion. Additionally, reverse signaling through PDL1 in tumor cells results in their resistance to death induced by cytotoxic T-lymphocytes (CTLs) and other mechanisms.
